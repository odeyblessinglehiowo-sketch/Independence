A Journey Through Time

Introduction

Before the Nigerian flag was first raised on October 1st, 1960, women like Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti and Margaret Ekpo were already fighting battles for equality and justice. Today, as we celebrate independence, we honor their courage, and pledge to continue building a Nigeria where every girl’s dream can rise as high as the eagle on our flag.

1960s – Nation Builders

Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti (25 October 1900 – 13 April 1978)

Called the “Lioness of Lisabi,” Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti was one of Nigeria’s fiercest advocates for justice. She challenged colonial taxation policies that oppressed women and rallied thousands of women to demand fairness. Her activism gave women a political voice at a time when society tried to silence them. She became a trailblazer for democracy, showing that Nigerian women could not only nurture homes but also defend nations. Funmilayo’s fight was not just for women in Abeokuta, but for the freedom and dignity of all Nigerians.

Margaret Ekpo (27 June 1914 – 21 September 2006)

Margaret Ekpo was more than a politician — she was a movement. At independence, she boldly entered the male-dominated political space and became a voice for women in leadership. She mobilized women in Aba, Enugu, and across Eastern Nigeria, proving that women were not just supporters of politics but active players in shaping the nation. She fought tirelessly for better wages, rights, and representation, setting the tone for women’s inclusion in governance.

Gambo Sawaba (15 February 1933 – 14 October 2001)

Gambo Sawaba (1933–2001) was a fearless northern Nigerian activist who defied tradition and colonial authority to demand justice for women. As a teenage organizer, she led bold campaigns for girls’ education, workers’ rights, and the right of northern women to vote, enduring repeated arrests and imprisonment for her activism. Her courage made her a powerful voice in the movement that carried Nigeria into independence.

1970s – Educators & Cultural Voices

Flora Nwapa (13 January 1931 – 16 October 1993)

When Flora Nwapa published Efuru in 1966, she became the first Nigerian female novelist to gain international recognition. In the 1970s, her writing gained momentum, telling stories that gave voice to women’s daily struggles, resilience, and triumphs. She challenged stereotypes, placing African women at the center of her narratives. Nwapa used literature as activism, proving that storytelling could reshape global perspectives on African womanhood.

Grace Alele Williams (16 December 1932 – 25 March 2022 )

Education in Nigeria took a historic turn when Grace Alele Williams broke barriers as the first female Vice-Chancellor of a Nigerian university (University of Benin). She became a symbol of possibility in academia, showing that women could lead institutions of learning and drive intellectual progress. Her leadership opened doors for countless young women who now saw education not as a limitation but as a ladder to the highest heights.

1980s – Breaking Barriers

Bola Kuforiji-Olubi (28 September 1936 – 3 December 2016)

Finance in the 1980s was a man’s world until Bola Kuforiji-Olubi shattered the glass ceiling by becoming the first female chairman of a Nigerian bank. She navigated the complexities of economics with brilliance and poise, proving that leadership was about competence, not gender. Her courage inspired a new wave of women professionals to dream of boardrooms, not just back offices.

Dora Akunyili (14 July 1954 – 7 June 2014

Dora Akunyili became the Director-General of NAFDAC and took on Nigeria’s counterfeit drug problem head-on. Facing threats and even assassination attempts, she stood firm, dismantling fake drug cartels and saving lives. Her leadership restored trust in Nigeria’s public health system and gave citizens hope that integrity could exist in government.

1990s – Democracy & Global Influence

Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala (13 June 1954)

The 1990s ushered in a woman whose name would echo across the world. Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala rose through the ranks at the World Bank, where she shaped global policy and development financing. She became living proof that Nigerian women could compete and lead at the highest levels of international economics. Her brilliance, integrity, and results-oriented leadership carved a new space for African women on the global stage.

Maryam Babangida (November 1, 1948 – December 27, 2009)

As First Lady, Maryam Babangida championed rural women through her Better Life for Rural Women program. She shifted the spotlight from elite politics to the grassroots, empowering rural women with access to resources, training, and visibility. Her work gave dignity to women often overlooked, reminding the nation that true development must include the villages as much as the cities.

2000s – Integrity, Business & Reforms

Oby Ezekwesili (28 April 1963)

Nicknamed “Madam Due Process,” Oby Ezekwesili brought accountability to Nigeria’s financial system. As a reformer, she reshaped procurement and introduced transparency into government spending. She later co-founded Transparency International and served as World Bank VP, cementing her place as a champion of justice and equity in governance.

Folorunsho Alakija (5 July 1951)

A true empire builder, Folorunsho Alakija transitioned from fashion into oil and gas, eventually becoming one of the richest women in Africa. But her wealth was not just about luxury , she invested in philanthropy, education, and mentorship, inspiring Nigerian women to dream audaciously. Her story became proof that women could create, own, and grow billion-dollar industries.

2010s – Global Icons, Media & Social Movements

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie (15 September 1977)

With novels like Half of a Yellow Sun and her iconic TED Talk “We Should All Be Feminists,” Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie became the global voice of Nigerian feminism. She used her art and platform to redefine gender narratives, challenging stereotypes about Africa and womanhood. She showed the world that storytelling is power and Nigerian women have stories worth telling.

Amina Mohammed (27 June 1961)

From Nigeria to the UN, Amina Mohammed’s journey inspired millions. As UN Deputy Secretary-General, she drove global policy on climate change, poverty reduction, and sustainable development. She became the face of global diplomacy, representing not only Nigeria but also the potential of African women to lead on world issues.

Hadiza Bala Usman (2 January 1976)

When Boko Haram kidnapped over 200 Chibok girls, Hadiza Bala Usman co-founded the Bring Back Our Girls campaign. Her activism mobilized global outrage and gave the kidnapped girls’ families a voice. She later became MD of the Nigerian Ports Authority, proving that activism and governance can go hand in hand.

Mo Abudu (11 September 1964)

Nicknamed “The Oprah of Africa,” Mo Abudu revolutionized African storytelling through EbonyLife Media. She created a platform for Nigerian films, TV, and culture to shine on global stages like Netflix. She gave Africa a voice in media, proving that representation matters and that Nigerian women could tell their own stories to the world.

2020s – Tech & Innovation

Funke Opeke (1961)

Funke Opeke, founder of MainOne, laid the foundation for Nigeria’s digital revolution by building undersea cables that connected West Africa to the world. She showed that tech infrastructure was not just for men, and she became a giant in a space where few women had dared to go.

Odunayo Eweniyi (1992)

Co-founder of PiggyVest and Feminist Coalition, Odunayo is the face of a new generation of women blending fintech and activism. She’s helping young Nigerians save and invest while also fighting for social justice. Odunayo represents a new Nigeria: bold, digital, and justice-driven.

Future Forward – Governance & Empowerment (G4EP Era)

Hon. Dr. Mrs. Judith Ogbara

As Chairman of G4EP, Dr. Judith Ogbara stands as a transformative leader bridging grassroots empowerment with national policy. She has personally sponsored over 1,000 female students in Akwa Ibom State by covering WAEC and JAMB forms, offered business grants to 50 women entrepreneurs, and disbursed start-up funds to outstanding young women entering higher education.
Her leadership ensures that women and girls not only receive opportunities but are also equipped to sustain them. Through her advisory role in the Gender Education, Engagement, Empowerment, and Entrepreneurship Program (GEEEEP), she is mentoring Nigeria’s next generation of female leaders.
Dr. Ogbara symbolizes the bold, hands-on empowerment that rises from local communities and scales into national change.

Her Excellency Senator Oluremi Tinubu (21 September 1960)

Her Excellency Senator Oluremi Tinubu – Nigeria’s First Lady and matriarch of national service. Through the Renewed Hope Initiative (RHI), she is empowering women entrepreneurs, supporting widows, and creating safety nets for vulnerable groups. With decades in the Senate and now as First Lady, she embodies faith, resilience, and mentorship for women in governance.
Together, Ogbara and Tinubu symbolize the bridge between Nigeria’s independence legacy and its empowered future.
